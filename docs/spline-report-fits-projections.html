Report on the stochastic model for Georgia with the inclusion of a latent trend in transmission rate

Andrew Tredennick

13 May, 2020

Overview

The current Georgia stochostic model of COVID-19 tranmission incorporates a metric of human movement (\(\phi(t)\)) that moderates the baseline tranmission rate (\(\beta\)): \(\beta(t) = \phi(t)\beta\). This formulation is meant to represent the impact of social distancing. Social distancing, while powerful, is not the only practice that can reduce transmission. Other practices, such as wearing face coverings, maintaining social distance in public spaces, and enhanced cleaning practices, can also reduce transmission. These practices are difficult to quantify in data and, in turn, are difficult to model explicitly. Nonetheless, we expect that mobility data will become less informative of transmission over time and that other practices will play a larger role.

I used a basis function approach (spline modeling) to model a latent temporal process that mediates baseline tranmission rate. The idea is to include a smooth temporal function in the model that stands in for all unobserved processes that can reduce transmission.

Here I report on models fit to the most recent data and projections from the fitted models. In particular, I am interested in the following things:

  1. Variability in the latent trend across MLE sets
  2. Variability in other parameter values across MLE sets
  3. Model fit to data
  4. Scenario projections

Results

Variability in the latent trend across MLE sets

Variability among the estimated latent trends is high early in the time series, when cases, hospitalizations, and deaths were all low or NA. Once the data become more informative, about half way through March, the trends become more similar.

Scenario projections

These are just some first stabs at scenario projections for the model – we’ll need to brainstorm the best approach here. I excluded the “what it social distancing had continued to increase?” counter-factual, for now. Here I list the scenarios and how they were implemented. Many ignore the latent altogether by simply repeating the final value from the observed period into the future.

  1. No intervention: set both \(\phi\) and \(\psi\) to one, such that baseline transmission is used throughout the simulation
  2. Return to normal: both \(\phi\) and \(\psi\) return to one over a seven day period and then remain there.
  3. All others: \(\phi\) is perturbed as per the “regular” model and \(\psi\) is set to the last estimated value.

I am just going to plot the log-scale projections.

Conclusions

I am relatively happy and comfortable with these results. The one thing that gives me pause are the relatively high estimates of \(\beta\) (baseline transmission). This results in very rapid upward trajectories under, for example, the “return to normal” scenario. In part, this is because I am currently assuming that the latent trend can be interpreted solely as a representation of human actions. Thus, returning it to 1 retrieves the “no human action” baseline tranmission rate. Of course, the latent trend incorporates all sorts of things! So simply setting it to 1 (or some other arbitrary number) reflects more than just changes in human actions/interventions.

Implementation

We currently model the force of infection (\(f\)) at time \(t\) as:

\[ f(t) = \phi(t)\frac{\beta}{N} (I(t)), \]

where, for simplicity, \(I\) stands for all infectious individuals at time \(t\). The mobility index \(\phi\) looks like this:

I proposed to add a temporal basis function to incorporate additional tranmission reduction over time that we cannot observe. Let the new temporal term be \(\psi\), which is modeled as:

\[ \text{logit}\left(\psi(t)\right) = \sum_{i=1}^K q_i \xi_{i_t}, \]

where \(K\) is the number of knots, \(\mathbf{q}\) is a vector of spline coefficients (to be fitted), and \(\mathbf{\xi}\) is a matrix basis functions. I define the number of knots (\(K\)) as the number of days in the data set divided by 7 (so, one knot per week). Note the logit transformation to go from the linear scale to the 0 - 1 scale. Thus, \(f(t)\) becomes: \(f(t) = \psi(t)\phi(t)\frac{\beta}{N} (I(t))\).
